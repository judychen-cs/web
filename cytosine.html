Cytosine

Structure

Cytosine, like thymine, is also a pyrimidine. One cytosine molecule consists of four carbons, five hydrogens, three nitrogens, and one oxygen. When it is in the form of cytidine triphosphate, it has multiple abilities: acting as an enzymatic cofactor, or transferring an extra phosphate group to adenosine diphosphate (ADP) to adenosine triphosphate (ATP).

Cytosine's Methylation

This specific nitrogenous base brings the talk of epigenetics to the table. Epigenetics is the study of heritable phenotypic anomalies that do not involve a change in DNA sequence, usually as a result of the environment. During the process of methylation, methyl groups can attach to cytosine, causing gene silencing; the specific gene or sequence impacted by methylation can no longer be transcribed.

In addition to the thymine dimers, cytosine methylation can also cause cancer manifestation.
